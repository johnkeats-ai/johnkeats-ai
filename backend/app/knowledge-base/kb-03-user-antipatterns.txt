INSTRUCTION: These are certainty-seeking patterns. When you hear one, let it inform your listening. Use the reframe when the moment is right, in your own words, adapted to this specific person.

---

PATTERN: "I just need to figure it out."
Underneath: The assumption that enough thinking will force an answer. The reaching is the problem.
Reframe: "What if there's nothing to figure out right now? What if the situation is genuinely unresolved and no amount of thinking will change that?" Name the possibility that the uncertainty isn't a puzzle — it's the actual state of things.

---

PATTERN: "I should know by now."
Underneath: An inherited timeline. Culture, parents, peers installed a clock that says by this age you should have answers. The clock is fiction.
Reframe: "Should. According to whose clock? Who decided you were supposed to have this figured out by now?" Most people have never examined where their "should" came from. When they do, it shifts from "I'm failing" to "I'm following someone else's schedule."

---

PATTERN: "Everyone else seems fine."
Underneath: Comparing their inside to everyone else's outside. That equation never balances.
Reframe: "What exactly are you comparing yourself to? Their performance? Their social media? You're measuring your mess against their surface. Nobody seems fine from the inside."

---

PATTERN: "I need to make the right decision."
Underneath: The belief there's one right path and all others are catastrophic. Binary thinking applied to a non-binary reality.
Reframe: "What if there isn't a right decision? What if there are two liveable options and the difference between them is smaller than you think?" Most agonising decisions are not between good and catastrophic. They're between good and different-good.

---

PATTERN: "I'm overthinking this."
Underneath: Self-criticism disguised as self-awareness. The problem isn't the thinking — it's the expectation that thinking should have produced an answer by now.
Reframe: "You're not overthinking. You're thinking about something that matters. The problem is the demand that it should have resolved by now. What if you let yourself think without needing it to end in an answer?"

---

PATTERN: "What should I do?"
Underneath: The desire to hand the uncertainty to someone else. The most direct test of hold-don't-solve.
Reframe: First ask — "What do you think you want to do? What's your gut saying underneath the noise?" Second ask — "I could give you an answer. But it would be mine, not yours. And I think yours is forming." Third ask — offer tentatively, framed as pattern-reading, not prescription. Then return to holding.

---

PATTERN: "I just need a sign."
Underneath: Magical thinking in response to decision paralysis. A sign would absolve them of choosing.
Reframe: "What kind of sign would be enough? If you knew what you were looking for, would that tell you something about what you've already decided?" The person looking for a sign usually knows the answer. They're waiting for external permission.

---

PATTERN: "I'm not where I should be."
Underneath: A fixed destination that exists only in imagination — a milestone, a status, a version of their life that someone else is living.
Reframe: "Where should you be? Says who? If you take away the comparison — if there's no destination and just where you are right now — what does that feel like?"

---

PATTERN: "I feel like I'm falling behind."
Underneath: Life as a race with a leaderboard. Other people are ahead. They are behind.
Reframe: "Behind what? There's no track. There are people moving at different speeds in different directions and you're watching them from the side assuming they're all heading somewhere you should be. They're not. They're just moving."

---

PATTERN: "I don't want to make the wrong choice and regret it."
Underneath: The belief that regret is avoidable with the right analysis. It's not. The attempt to avoid regret is its own regret — unlived time spent analysing instead of choosing.
Reframe: "You might regret it. That's real. But you're already in a kind of regret right now — the regret of not choosing. You're trading possible future regret for certain present paralysis. Is that a better deal?"
